Chapter 28 - NEVER FORCE ANYTHING (Tareq)

I once hired a camera crew for a day long filing of some exercises.

The lead camera man, and owner of the camera equipment was a man named Tareq. He was sharp, witty and always very calculated with his shoots. As with any camera owners, he was very particular with how to carry things and what someone who wasn’t part of gis team could carry. He also had a very swift way of operating that seemed quite seamless. A lot of the tims he seemed to do things just once. And well.

As we were preparing the yoga studio for filming, I went to open some large sliding doors. I gave them a yank with my arm and jerked them slightly open. They were unlocked and they did open, and I used some brute force town style technique similar to how I would usually lift a dumbbell or do a pull up. Tareq walked over to the second door, gazed up at it/ and said “Never force anything”. In the fitness industry, you get pretty used to forcing, throwing and biffing things around, with enough accuracy to rerack a weight or put a medicine ball back in place. In Tareq’s world, you never threw anything, and you never carried “(his brightest light).

When we are training, we need to put our bodies through stress to adapt. We need to “force” our bodies to change. But we might not need to force things beyond what will make a difference. Have you cmpelteted your training session? Do you need to force another set for the sake of doing more reps? Do you need to squeeze in a cardio session today when you’re already fatigued or can you do it 12 hours later (in the morning) after a nights rest? Are you struggling to nail the technique of a dance drill, ball skill or drum beat? Try less, force it less, take a break, give it some space.

Whenever we watch athletes completing incredible feats of strength, skill or power, they often have similar qualities of a person who is effortlessly buttering their toast. No wasted energy, relaxed and completely in tune with the task. There is force behind it, but nothing more. And more would not be better.

When you know how much effort is required to achieve something, why do more? Is that lazy, or is a calculated approach? Is doing more a status symbol? There are many examples in day to day life where fracking something is a recipe for mistakes. Pouring milk into cereal too fast, buttoning up a shirt, putting your toe socks on too fast, skipping part of your running warm up or having a drink of water.

Gravity and physics have a quiet and firm grip and the final say on whether something cna be forced or not. And with exercise, sometimes it’s going to take as long as it takes, and sometimes it’s ok to finish a set on an odd number, a run at 9.9 kilometers, a lift a 97.5kg or a dance move unfinished. Even if you feel fresh, don’t wear forceful actions as a must.

See time as your friend, never force anything.
